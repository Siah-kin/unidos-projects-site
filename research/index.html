Unidos Research

Nakivale Refugee Settlement, Uganda

Can $10 a month change a life?

Two research projects. One question. We are testing whether a small, steady income - combined with coaching, cooperation, and good data - can help a refugee build a livelihood from scratch.

Research Phase: We are running these studies with real farmers in Nakivale. Results are shared with members as data comes in. What you see below is the framework, the math, and the honest gaps we still need to close.

Research 1: The $10 Threshold

We believe $10 per month in passive income is the minimum amount that shifts a person from survival mode into investment mode. We call this the activation threshold.

The E-motional Gauge

This is not just about money. It is about what becomes possible at each level. Below $10, a person spends everything on staying alive. At $10, they can start building something.

Monthly IncomeWhat it feels likeWhat becomes possible
$0 Stuck. Dependent on food rations. No choices. Nothing. Wait for help or go without.
$5 Surviving, not building. Can eat but not invest. Buy food. Maybe medicine. No surplus.
$10 The turning point. Enough to plant a seed. Buy $8 of mushroom spawn + $2 compost materials. Or buy medicine to get healthy enough to work. Or buy clothes for a job interview. The first real choice.
$25 Building momentum. Reinvesting surplus. Scale production. Start a second crop. Help a neighbor get started.
$100 Community anchor. Others look to you. Hire help. Pool orders for better prices. Teach what you know.
$500 Graduated. Ready for formal credit. Open a savings group account. Apply for a microfinance loan. No longer dependent on aid.

The compounding cycle

Can someone who gets $10/month passively compound it into $500 in savings within a year? Here is how the math works in Nakivale, using mushroom cultivation as the example:

MonthInvestActionRevenueSaved

2 $10 + $4 reinvest Buy 5 spawn bags, expand growing area 25kg = $24 $38

12 $10 + compound Established operation. Selling to buyers directly. ~$100/mo $550+

Why this works in Nakivale

Land is allocated by UNHCR - not purchased. Compost is made from organic waste - free. Water is available at settlement boreholes. Training - Unidos already taught 501 people exactly these techniques. The bottleneck was never knowledge. It was the first $10 to buy inputs.

What kills it without help

A wholesaler drives in, offers half the market price. The farmer has no price reference. Accepts. Revenue drops 43%. Or: the farmer has surplus mushrooms but no buyer. They spoil in 2 days. Total loss. Or: a health emergency wipes savings back to zero.

This is why the compounding cycle needs three things beyond the $10: price information (what are mushrooms actually selling for today?), buyer connections (who needs 25kg this week?), and a safety floor (the passive $10 that prevents total reset after a bad month).

The pilot: 10 people, 12 months

We want to coach 10 farmers through this cycle and track everything: what they plant, what they harvest, what they sell, what they save, and what goes wrong. Not a controlled experiment in a lab - a real test with real people facing real problems.

Specific
10 coached farmers in Nakivale. Mushroom cultivation as primary crop. Bot-assisted price information and buyer matching.
Measurable
Gauge level ($0-$500), kilograms harvested, sale prices achieved, savings accumulated, cooperation activity logged.
Achievable
Unidos already trained 501 farmers. Spawn bags cost $2-3. Harvest cycle is 3-4 weeks. The skills and inputs exist.
Relevant
130,000 people in Nakivale. 67% live on $1-2/day casual labor. 82% are unbanked. This is the population the research serves.
Time-bound
12 months. Checkpoints: month 3 ($120 saved), month 6 ($280 saved), month 12 ($500+ saved).

Research 2: Turning helpful data into economic opportunity

When a farmer uses a tool to check prices, list products, or answer a neighbor's question about compost - that activity generates data. We are researching whether that data can serve as a form of collateral to unlock small loans, better prices, and community credit - without needing a bank account or government ID.

How cooperation becomes trust

The farmer is already doing the work - planting, harvesting, answering a neighbor's question about compost ratios, reporting what mushrooms sold for at the market this week. We track patterns in that activity:

  • Frequency - How often does this person contribute useful information?
  • Quality - Do their price reports match what other users report? Do their answers help?
  • Consistency - Do they show up week after week, or only when they need something?
  • Reciprocity - Do they help others as much as they ask for help?

Over time, these patterns produce a trust profile. Not a credit score from a bank - a behavioral record built from real activity the person already does.

What a trust profile unlocks

The principle: your own behavior already proved you are trustworthy. Here is what that unlocks:

  • Better prices - Trusted sellers get priority in listings. Buyers see their track record before negotiating.
  • Input credit - A farmer with 30 verified interactions gets spawn bags on credit. Someone with 5 does not. The threshold is transparent.
  • Peer vouching - A trusted farmer can vouch for a newcomer. If the newcomer defaults, both records reflect it. Trust is shared, not just individual.
  • Bulk access - Farmers with consistent records pool orders for fertilizer or transport, cutting costs that no individual can negotiate alone.

The closed loop

What makes this different from other research: the data is the product AND the proof. The farmer is not filling out surveys for researchers. They are using a tool that helps them sell mushrooms - and the usage data itself becomes three things at once:

1. Their credit record - proving trustworthiness without a bank ID

2. A research dataset - agricultural knowledge, market prices, cooperation patterns (anonymized, never individual identities exposed)

3. A revenue stream - organizations pay for aggregate insights, and that revenue funds the next farmer's spawn bags

Three outputs from one activity. That is the closed loop.

The bigger picture: data as collateral for community loans

We collect anonymized, privacy-protected data in a shared pool. This pool becomes a form of collateral - proof that a community is productive, cooperative, and creditworthy. Against that collateral, we can issue small loans to give new projects a kick-start budget. When the farmer repays after harvest, the repayment adds to their trust profile, which unlocks better terms next time.

The long-term vision: these cooperation patterns, tracked over years, become a kind of heritage. A lineage of trustworthy behavior that compounds across seasons and across generations. The community builds something that belongs to them - not to a bank, not to an NGO, not to a platform.

Academic references

The two research projects above are grounded in existing peer-reviewed work. These are the primary sources:

  • Betts et al. (2022) - Exclusion of refugees from financial services. Survey of 5,000 refugees across 3 countries: 94% want bank accounts, 18% succeed. The barrier is paperwork, not willingness.
  • Pentland et al. (2023) - "New Deal on Data." Pilot in Kenya showed users valued their own data at $5-20/month. Foundation for our data-as-collateral pricing.
  • Ruddick et al. (2015) - Community currencies in Kenya: 15% increase in local trade, 22% improvement in food security over 4 years with 10,000 users.
  • Lanier & Weyl (2018) - Data dignity. When communities negotiate collectively for their data, they earn $20-50/user/year vs nearly nothing as individuals.
  • Ostrom (1990) - 8 principles for managing shared resources without top-down control. Our community governance model follows these principles.
  • Mondragon Cooperative (1956-present) - 70+ years of worker-owned governance with 80,000+ members. Proof that community-owned economies can last generations.
