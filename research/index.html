Unidos Research

Nakivale Refugee Settlement, Uganda

Data for Credit

Can transparent data exchange between an NGO and 500 farmers produce a self-sustaining input credit system without grant dependency?

Research phase. We are running this study with real farmers in Nakivale. What you see below is the framework, the math, and the honest gaps we still need to close.

The problem

A farmer in Nakivale refugee settlement harvests 20 kg of mushrooms. A wholesaler drives in from Mbarara, offers 2,000 UGX per kilogram. She accepts - she has no way to check the real price, and the bus to Mbarara costs $20, ten days of wages. The wholesaler resells at 3x in town.

This happens to 130,000 people, every week, and has for 70 years.

Unidos trains farmers in mushroom cultivation, vermicomposting, and biochar production inside Nakivale. 2024 output: 501 farmers trained, 4,800 kg mushrooms produced, 7.8 tons vermicompost processed.

67% of households still depend on casual labor at $1-2 per day. 27% have smartphones. No dedicated marketplace exists.

Can transparent data-for-credit exchange between an NGO and 500 farmers produce a self-sustaining input credit system without grant dependency by month 18?

How it works

Unidos deploys a Telegram bot. The farmer's daily activity becomes the data that funds the next farmer's credit.

What the farmer does

  • Farmer types /price mushrooms - bot returns last 5 Mbarara prices reported by other users that week
  • Farmer posts a produce listing to sell directly
  • Farmer answers a neighbor's composting question
  • Bot logs every interaction: question asked, answer given, trade completed, price reported

What the data becomes

Unidos sells the anonymized aggregate to agricultural researchers, carbon offset buyers, and AI training labs. No individual identities are exposed - only patterns: seasonal price trends, soil treatment outcomes, cooperation rates, crop yields by method.

Projected data revenue at 500 active users:

$2,250 per month

That revenue funds mushroom spawn bags and compost issued on credit to farmers with 5 or more logged bot interactions. Farmer repays after harvest at 20% surcharge. Repayment logs to their bot record - a transaction history they own.

Three outputs from one activity. The farmer checks prices to sell mushrooms better. That same usage (1) builds their credit record, (2) generates a research dataset, and (3) creates the revenue that funds the next farmer's inputs. Closed loop.

The credit ladder

The barrier is the first step, not creditworthiness. KIVA's own data shows 95%+ repayment from refugee borrowers.

1
5+ bot interactions

Questions, trades, price reports. No bank needed, no ID needed.

2
Unidos input credit

Spawn bags and compost on credit. Repay after harvest. Bot record is the only requirement.

3
VSLA group guarantee

Village Savings and Loan Association - bot record serves as evidence of activity and repayment history.

4
UGAFODE individual loan

Uganda's microfinance bank - the only formal lender operating inside Nakivale.

5
KIVA international

Refugees repay at 95%+. The bot record provides the track record that KIVA's model needs.

6
Opportunity Bank commercial credit

Full banking relationship. No longer dependent on aid or NGO credit.

Each step is harder to reach but worth more. The bot record at step 1 costs nothing to create. By step 6, the farmer has a documented financial history that any lender can verify.

The $10 threshold

We believe $10 per month in passive income is the minimum amount that shifts a person from survival mode into investment mode. Below $10, everything goes to staying alive. At $10, the first real choice appears.

What becomes possible at each level

Monthly incomeWhat it feels likeWhat becomes possible
$0 Stuck. Dependent on food rations. No choices. Nothing. Wait for help or go without.
$5 Surviving, not building. Can eat but not invest. Buy food. Maybe medicine. No surplus.
$10 The turning point. Enough to plant a seed. Buy $8 of mushroom spawn + $2 compost materials. First real choice.
$25 Building momentum. Reinvesting surplus. Scale production. Start a second crop. Help a neighbor get started.
$100 Community anchor. Others look to you. Hire help. Pool orders for better prices. Teach what you know.
$500 Graduated. Ready for formal credit. Open a savings group account. Apply for a microfinance loan. No longer dependent on aid.

Why this works in Nakivale

Land is allocated by UNHCR - not purchased. Compost is made from organic waste - free. Water is available at settlement boreholes. Training - Unidos already taught 501 people exactly these techniques. The bottleneck was never knowledge. It was the first $10 to buy inputs.

What kills it without help

A wholesaler drives in, offers half the market price. The farmer has no price reference. Accepts. Revenue drops 43%. Or: surplus mushrooms with no buyer. They spoil in 2 days. Total loss. Or: a health emergency wipes savings back to zero.

This is why the compounding cycle needs three things beyond the $10: price information (what are mushrooms selling for today?), buyer connections (who needs 25kg this week?), and a safety floor (the passive $10 that prevents total reset after a bad month).

The Telegram bot provides all three. And the usage data from providing those three services is what funds the credit for the next farmer.

Academic references

This research is grounded in existing peer-reviewed work. Primary sources:

  • Betts et al. (2022) - Exclusion of refugees from financial services. Survey of 5,000 refugees across 3 countries: 94% want bank accounts, 18% succeed. The barrier is paperwork, not willingness.
  • Pentland et al. (2023) - "New Deal on Data." Pilot in Kenya showed users valued their own data at $5-20/month. Foundation for our data-as-collateral pricing.
  • Ruddick et al. (2015) - Community currencies in Kenya: 15% increase in local trade, 22% improvement in food security over 4 years with 10,000 users.
  • Lanier & Weyl (2018) - Data dignity. When communities negotiate collectively for their data, they earn $20-50/user/year vs nearly nothing as individuals.
  • Ostrom (1990) - 8 principles for managing shared resources without top-down control. Our community governance model follows these principles.
  • Mondragon Cooperative (1956-present) - 70+ years of worker-owned governance with 80,000+ members. Proof that community-owned economies can last generations.
